The Practice

Organizations are investing heavily in commitments their structures are not always built to sustain.

They invest in culture initiatives, leadership development, employer brand campaigns, internal communications platforms, change management efforts, wellbeing programs, DEI strategies, ESG reporting, impact narratives, and transformation programs. These investments are extensive. The intentions behind them are typically genuine. The initiatives have been well-designed. The communications are clear and compelling.

Yet most still do not produce the adoption, credibility, or measurable return leaders expected.

The instinct is to create another program — or most often, improve the message. Better language. Stronger storytelling. More leadership visibility. More investment in the expression layer. It's not that the instinct is wrong. It's that it is almost always incomplete.

Every day, HR directors invest in employer brand and people commitments, communications leaders build internal campaigns, change managers craft transformation narratives, and nonprofit executives write grant narratives — with clear intent and rigorous craft. Most of those commitments are structured as programs and initiatives: well-designed, properly resourced, and launched with organizational support.

But programs cannot solve governance conditions.

Those investments frequently run ahead of the structural conditions that would make them credible — and without the governance infrastructure to hold them, they revert.

Compositions™ analyzes the structural conditions beneath organizational communications — governance, incentives, decision rights, manager routines, cultural norms, and evidence systems — before the distance between what organizations say and how they operate becomes damage. It designs the governance infrastructure that makes coherence between what organizations say, what they fund, and how they operate self-sustaining.

The Work

Not a communications agency. Not a culture consultancy. Not an HR advisory firm.

A structural advisory practice for organizations where investments in culture, change, brand, and people commitments are not producing the adoption, credibility, or measurable return leaders expected.

The issue is not the design of the initiative or the clarity of the communications. It is the structural gap between what the organization says it commits to and what its operating conditions can actually sustain.

The entry point is the communications problem.
The work is structural.
The outcome is credibility that compounds.

The deliverables are written structural reads and governance blueprints — designed for implementation by the organization's existing team, not to create dependency on continued outside support.

Why Compositions™

The name holds two meanings. Both describe the same problem.

In writing, music and design, a composition is not a collection of elements — it is the intentional arrangement of elements into a unified whole. The quality of the composition is not only determined by the quality of the individual pieces. It is determined by how they work together.

Organizations are compositions. They are arrangements of commitments, structures, incentives, communications, governance, and behavior. When those elements are arranged coherently — when the culture narrative aligns with what the incentive structure rewards, when the employer brand describes an experience the operating conditions produce, when change communications are grounded in the structural credibility required to make them believed — the organization is well-composed. The whole becomes credible.

When the elements contradict each other, the organization fragments. The individual pieces may be strong. The arrangement is producing incoherence.

Most communications work is about the quality of the message. My work is about whether people can believe it — and what it costs when they cannot.
The Second Meaning

A composition in this context is where structure meets communications — the relationship between what the organization is built to deliver and what it says it delivers.

Reading that relationship, identifying where it has separated from what the organization says and how it operates, and designing the conditions that make coherence self-sustaining is the work.

The entry point is visible: the communications problem — the employer brand that isn't converting, the change initiative that isn't holding, the internal campaign that isn't moving anyone. The structural source is beneath it. That is where the work goes.

The Foundation

Seven lenses. One analytical capacity no single discipline can match.

Most practitioners in the employer brand, internal communications, and change communications space hold one or two of these lenses. The work of Compositions™ is built from all seven simultaneously — which is why the structural read it produces is different in kind, not just in degree, from what adjacent advisory and consulting practices can offer.

01

Strategic Communications at Enterprise Scale

Reads the gap between what the organization says, what the operating architecture can support, and what stakeholders actually experience. The lens that makes every other discipline applicable — keeping structural analysis grounded in what organizations are actually doing, not only in what the research says about them.

02

Brand Strategy

Reads whether the brand promise — including the employer brand — is structurally real, or performing a reality the organization cannot currently sustain. This is how the Glassdoor gap is read not as a reputation problem but as a structural credibility problem with a specific, closeable source.

03

Organizational Development at Enterprise Scale

Reads the conditions shaping behavior at scale: decision rights, accountability, capability, role clarity, trust, and adaptation. Grounded in director-level roles at enterprise-scale organizations — not theoretical, operational.

04

Culture Integration at Enterprise Scale

Reads how operating decisions, incentives, leadership behavior, and norms either reinforce or quietly defeat organizational commitments — and what that means for whether communications about culture will be believed. Culture as a produced condition of governance, not a chosen attribute.

05

Impact & Outcomes Measurement

Reads whether the portfolio of commitments organizations are already funding is producing defensible outcomes. Connects investment, evidence, and stakeholder value in the capital language that makes structural arguments boardroom-legible — to funders, boards, and CFOs who want the number before they approve the case.

06

Social Epidemiology & Structural Determinant Thinking

Grounds the work in the principle that structural conditions, not intentions alone, shape outcomes across populations. The same theoretical framework that explains health disparities across communities explains why organizational commitments revert: the structure, not the program, determines the outcome.

07

Instructional Design & Permanent Capability Transfer

Ensures the work transfers into permanent internal capability. The governance architecture is designed to be owned and maintained by the organization — not to require ongoing outside interpretation to sustain it. The engagement ends. The coherence continues.

The question asked most often inside large organizations isn't:

"How do we communicate this commitment better?"

It is: "What structural conditions would need to be true for this commitment to be credible?"

That question was asked repeatedly over more than two decades of practice inside enterprise-scale organizations — building the communications infrastructure required to make people and culture commitments structurally real, across complex multi-office environments, and navigating the specific gap between what organizational leadership intends to commit to and what the governance, incentive, and operating architecture can actually sustain.

The answer kept pointing to the same place: governance, incentives, decision rights, and operating behavior — the conditions beneath the communications layer, not the communications themselves. That is where Compositions™ operates.

The practitioner who can most clearly see the structural source of the credibility gap is rarely the person with the authority to close it alone. Dana has spent her career in that position — and building the framework that changes what's possible from it.
