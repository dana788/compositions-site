The Practice

Compositions™ exists because organizations are investing more in programs and commitments than the structural conditions beneath them can support.

Every day, HR directors invest in employer brand and people commitments, communications leaders build internal campaigns, change managers craft transformation narratives, and nonprofit executives write grant narratives — with genuine intent and genuine craft.

But the pattern is consistent: sophisticated audiences do not evaluate communications. They measure it against operating evidence they already hold. They know what gets rewarded. They have seen what happens when commitments meet budget pressure. They have noticed the distance between what leadership says and what the operating decisions confirm.

When that distance is small, communications produces belief. When it is large, no investment in message quality closes it.

Compositions™ analyzes the structural conditions beneath organizational communications — governance, incentives, decision rights, manager routines, cultural norms, and evidence systems — before that distance becomes damage. It designs the governance infrastructure that makes coherence between what organizations say, what they fund, and how they operate self-sustaining.

The Work

Compositions™ is not a communications agency. Not a culture consultancy. Not an HR advisory firm.

It is a structural advisory practice for organizations where investments in culture, change, brand, and people commitments are not producing the adoption, credibility, or measurable return leaders expected.

The issue is rarely the quality of the initiative or the clarity of the message. It is the structural gap between what the organization is funding, what it is communicating, and what its operating conditions can actually sustain.

The entry point is the communications problem. The work is structural. The outcome is credibility that compounds.

The deliverables are written structural reads and governance blueprints — designed for implementation by the organization's existing team, not to create dependency on continued outside support.

Why Compositions™

The name holds two meanings. Both describe the same problem.

In music and design, a composition is not a collection of elements — it is the intentional arrangement of elements into a unified whole. The quality of the composition is not only determined by the quality of the individual pieces. It is determined by how they work together.

Organizations are compositions. They are arrangements of commitments, structures, incentives, communications, governance, and behavior. When those elements are arranged coherently — when the culture narrative aligns with what the incentive structure rewards, when the employer brand describes an experience the operating conditions produce, when change communications is grounded in the structural credibility required to make it believed — the organization is well-composed. The whole becomes credible.

When the elements contradict each other, the organization fragments. The individual pieces may be strong. The arrangement is producing incoherence.

The second meaning: composition is where structure meets communications — the relationship between what the organization is built to deliver and what it says it delivers. Reading that relationship, identifying where it has separated, and designing the conditions that make coherence self-sustaining is the work.

Most communications work is about the quality of the message. My work is about whether people can believe it.

The Foundation

The lens is built from seven disciplines most advisors treat as separate domains.

Each discipline sees a different dimension of the same structural problem. Together, they produce an analytical capacity no single discipline can match — and that no other practitioner in the employer brand, internal communications, and change communications space currently holds.

Strategic Communications at Enterprise Scale

Reads the gap between what the organization says, what the operating architecture can support, and what stakeholders actually experience. Operates across governance, strategic, execution, and capability transfer layers simultaneously.

Brand Strategy

Reads whether the brand promise — including the employer brand — is structurally real, or performing a reality the organization cannot currently sustain.

Organizational Development at Enterprise Scale

Reads the conditions shaping behavior at scale: decision rights, accountability, capability, role clarity, trust, and adaptation. Grounded in director-level roles across a 550-person global agency and a 1,800-employee regional health system.

Culture Integration at Enterprise Scale

Reads how operating decisions, incentives, leadership behavior, and norms either reinforce or quietly defeat organizational commitments — and what that means for whether communications about culture will be believed.

Impact & Outcomes Measurement

Reads whether investments in programs and commitments are producing defensible outcomes — connecting investment, evidence, and stakeholder value in ways that make structural arguments credible to funders, boards, and senior leadership.

Social Epidemiology & Structural Determinant Thinking

Grounds the work in the principle that structural conditions, not intentions alone, shape outcomes across populations.

Instructional Design & Permanent Capability Transfer

Ensures the work transfers into permanent internal capability — so organizations don't require ongoing outside interpretation to sustain the governance over time.

Dana Middleton | Structural Communications Advisor

Dana Middleton is a Structural Communications Advisor and the principal of Compositions™, a Kansas City-based advisory practice. She works with mid-market organizations and nonprofits on a problem the field rarely names directly: the distance between what an organization is funding, what it is communicating, and what its operating conditions can credibly sustain.

The work grew from a question asked repeatedly over more than two decades of practice inside enterprise-scale organizations. Not "how do we communicate this commitment better?" but "what structural conditions would need to be true for this commitment to be credible?" The answer kept pointing to the same place: governance, incentives, decision rights, and operating behavior — the conditions beneath the communications layer, not the communications itself.

With over two decades of experience across strategic communications, brand strategy, and enterprise-scale culture integration, Dana brings the pattern recognition of a practitioner who has been inside the gap, built the infrastructure to close it, and developed the structural framework to name it precisely.

She holds a Master's in Instructional Design, and her doctoral research examines coherence as a structural determinant of stakeholder well-being and enterprise health in organizations — the same question the practice answers in every engagement.
