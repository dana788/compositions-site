In 2025, for the first time in the history of Edelman's annual Trust Barometer, trust in employers declined globally. The institution that held the highest trust ranking since 2021 — outperforming government, media, and NGOs — dropped 3 points. Seventy-five percent of employees still trust their employer to do what is right. Twenty-five percent don't.

Taken together, those numbers may seem to warrant communications. The operating logic is nearly automatic: trust is declining, so more messaging. More transparency. More visible leadership. More authentic storytelling.

That is the wrong structural read.

Across the organizations I've analyzed, trust data rarely measures what leaders think it measures. Employees are not evaluating the quality of communications when they respond to a trust survey. They are reporting the distance between what organizational communications describes and what their operating environment confirms. That distance is what produces skepticism.

This distinction matters because it determines where the investment goes next.

## What Employees Are Actually Measuring

When an employee reads an internal communication about organizational values, they don't evaluate it against its clarity or craft. They evaluate it against the last promotion decision. The last budget reallocation. The last moment when stated commitments met operating pressure, and one of the two had to yield.

The Glassdoor review that doesn't reflect the employer brand: it is not a measurement problem. It is the evidence speaking at a higher volume than the communications.

Gallup's 2025 State of the Global Workplace report found that global employee engagement fell to its lowest level since the pandemic — 21% of employees engaged worldwide, with disengagement costing the global economy approximately $10 trillion in lost productivity. That figure represents more than dissatisfied workers. It represents the aggregate cost of organizations asking their people to sustain commitment to conditions that the organization is not sustaining in return.

The specific pattern is instructive: engagement scores peaked in 2022 and 2023\. They have declined in every subsequent year. In the same period, most large organizations increased their investment in employee communications — platforms, leadership visibility programs, culture campaigns, and values workshops. The investment went up. The belief did not.

This is not a coincidence. It is a structural pattern.

## The Operating Evidence Problem

Here is what employees already hold before any communication reaches them.

They know which behaviors the organization actually rewards — independent of what any values statement says. They track which commitments survived the last budget pressure and which did not. They have read the exit interviews of the colleagues who left. They have seen which leaders get the most sponsorship for advancement and what those leaders have in common. They know what the workload architecture produces, regardless of what the flexibility policy describes.

This body of operating evidence is not cynicism. It is organizational intelligence — the accumulated, firsthand record of what the organization actually does when its stated commitments and its operating incentives diverge. Employees build this record continuously. They update it with every decision the organization makes.

When an internal communication arrives, employees automatically run it against this record. Not deliberately, not adversarially — structurally. The communication either confirms what the operating evidence says, or describes a version of organizational reality that the operating evidence cannot confirm. In the latter case, the communication produces skepticism regardless of its quality.

This is why 68% of employees globally, in the 2025 Edelman Trust Barometer, believe business leaders deliberately mislead them — a 12-point increase from 2021\. The perception is almost certainly not accurate in most cases. Leaders are not, by and large, intentionally misleading their employees. But the gap between organizational communications and operating conditions can lead to a reading of deception even when deception is not the intent. The conditions are making the communications appear dishonest.

## Why Leadership Visibility Compounds the Problem

One of the most consistent responses to declining trust scores is increased leadership visibility — more all-hands presentations, more CEO video messages, more authentic storytelling in internal channels.

This investment in leadership presence makes intuitive sense. It is also, when deployed in the presence of a structural credibility gap, precisely the wrong move.

Leadership visibility without structural credibility consistency does not repair trust. It accelerates erosion. When employees receive more communications from a leadership team whose operating decisions regularly contradict those communications, the increased volume reads as evidence of the gap, not the resolution of it. The CEO's video message about organizational values lands differently after a budget decision that contradicted those values. The all-hands meeting about the importance of "our people" doesn't produce belief after the utilization targets that make the statement structurally implausible.

Manager engagement data from Gallup adds the mechanism layer. Manager engagement dropped nine points between 2022 and 2025, with the sharpest single-year decline occurring between 2024 and 2025, when it fell from 27% to 22%. Managers are the primary conduit through which organizational communications become lived experience for employees. When managers carry the structural contradiction — asked to communicate organizational commitments that the conditions of their own work do not confirm — the communications they deliver are filtered through that structural gap before they reach anyone on their team.

More leadership communication through that conduit does not close the gap. It adds volume to the signal of incoherence.

## The Investment Landing on the Wrong Variable

Most organizational responses to trust and engagement data are communications investments: better platforms, more frequent touchpoints, more sophisticated content, more visible leadership. These are reasonable responses if the problem is the quality or reach of communications. They are expensive non-solutions if the problem is structural coherence — the distance between what the organization communicates and what its operating conditions can confirm.

The data on this is consistent. In the four years since engagement levels peaked globally, communications investment across most mid-market organizations has grown rather than contracted. The belief deficit has grown alongside it.

What this pattern reveals is not that the communications investments were wrong — the execution was fine. It reveals that they were designed to solve a different problem than the one producing the outcomes. You can close a communications gap by investing in communications. You cannot close a structural one the same way. When the source of skepticism is the distance between what organizational communications describes and what its operating evidence confirms, that source is structural, not editorial.

What determines whether people believe communications is not how it is crafted, but whether the structural conditions it describes are real. When those conditions are coherent with what is communicated, belief compounds over time. When they diverge, the architecture fragments. Skepticism becomes self-reinforcing — and the more investment that goes into communications quality, the more precisely employees can read the distance between what the communications describes and what the operating evidence confirms.

This is why organizations that invest in employer branding, rebuild the EVP, upgrade their internal communications platform, and increase leadership visibility can still exhibit the same 18-month turnover pattern. The investment was genuine, but the structural conditions were not yet in place to support what the messaging was designed to convey.

## What Structural Coherence Produces

There is an observable difference in organizations where communications are structurally grounded. Employees in those organizations don't describe communications as polished or sophisticated. They describe it as accurate. The communications confirm what they already experience. The advancement criteria align with what the communications say the organization values. The utilization targets align with what the wellbeing communications say about a sustainable pace. The promotion decisions match what the values document says the organization rewards.

Trust in those organizations doesn't require investment in the quality of communications. It is compounded from structural consistency.

When the structural conditions and the communications describe the same organizational reality, belief accumulates without effort. When they diverge, no communications investment can close the distance — because employees are not reading the communications. They are reading the gap.

This is the structural read that clarifies the investment decision. It is not a question of whether to communicate more or better. It is a question of whether the structural conditions the communications are designed to express are arranged to support what is being communicated.

Did your organization's last communications push align with your operating decisions?
